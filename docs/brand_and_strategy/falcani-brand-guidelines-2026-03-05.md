# falcani: Brand Guidelines & Voice Manual

> This document is the single source of truth for falcani's brand identity, voice, visual system, and communication standards. It is designed to be consumed by both humans and AI tools. Any AI system using this guide must produce output that sounds like a real person wrote it, not a language model.

## 1. Who We Are

### Manifesto

We are falcani. We don't do bureaucracy. We solve problems.

In a world drowning in corporate complexity, we chose simplicity. Where others build hierarchies, we build solutions. We believe every business, regardless of size, deserves software that works, not PowerPoints about software that might work.

We are solvers, not managers. Creators, not committees. We speak human, not corporate. And we transform the impossible into the inevitable.

But here's the thing: we're not rebels for the sake of it. We care deeply about the businesses we work with. Every system we build, every process we simplify, every workflow we untangle, it's because someone on the other end of that screen is trying to grow something they believe in. We're here to make sure technology helps them do that, not get in the way.

### What falcani Is

A custom software factory for small and medium businesses. We build tailored software solutions for companies that have outgrown spreadsheets and generic tools but don't need (or want) the bloat of enterprise vendors.

We are not a consulting firm. We don't sell hours or slide decks. We build working software that solves specific problems.

### What falcani Is Not

We're not a generic SaaS vendor reselling someone else's platform. We don't operate like a freelancer marketplace where quality varies week to week. Consulting firms deliver recommendations instead of working code; that's not us either. And enterprise IT providers? They drown small businesses in the kind of complexity we exist to eliminate.

### Who We Work With

We do not filter by industry. A logistics company drowning in spreadsheets and a healthcare clinic stitching together five tools that do not talk to each other have the same core problem. We filter by honesty: can we actually solve this? If the answer is yes, we are in. If it is no, we will say so before anyone signs anything.

We work best with SMEs, typically 50 to 500 employees, that have outgrown their tools but have not outgrown their ability to move fast. The common thread is not what they sell; it is that their current systems are slowing them down, and they know it.

### Vision

Every small business should be able to compete with the big players, not by becoming big and bureaucratic, but by being smart and well-equipped.

### Mission

Be the trusted software factory that delivers well-designed, high-performance, tailored software, so small businesses can focus on what they actually do best.

### Origin

The name falcani is built from the initials of a family: Felix (father), Adelaida (mother), Luis (founder), Camilo (brother), Nicolas (nephew). The idea came from Adelaida herself, walking through the streets of Zipaquirá, Colombia on a regular afternoon. She looked at Luis and said, what if you use our initials to form the name? That was it. No branding agency, no naming workshop, no focus group. A mother, her son, and a word that didn't exist until she said it.

Luis Miguel Guzman Perez, the founder, spent eight years in the aerospace and defense industry. Aeronautical engineer by training. He saw something that most people in his field didn't: the small and medium businesses supplying the aerospace supply chain were running on broken processes, duct-taped tools, and manual workflows that nobody had time to fix. The big software vendors didn't care about companies that size. The consultants cared, but only enough to sell a deck and leave.

In December 2022, he jumped. No safety net, no venture backing, no client pipeline. Just a conviction that he could build better software for the businesses that actually needed it. He taught himself to code at a pace most people would call unreasonable, completed a master's degree in software engineering while building the company, and turned that conviction into a working business.

falcani carries its origin in every decision it makes. The name is a family. The approach is personal. The standards come from an industry where sloppy work can cost lives. And the refusal to overcomplicate things? That comes from watching an entire industry drown in complexity that nobody asked for.

## 2. Core Values

### Radical Simplification
Complexity is a choice. We choose clarity. Every line of code, every conversation, every solution: reduced to what matters. If a process has unnecessary steps, we kill them. If a feature doesn't solve a real problem, we don't build it.

### Anti-Hierarchical Collaboration
Every team member at falcani is a "solver." No corporate titles, no power plays, no communication chains that add delay without adding value. Ideas win on merit, not org chart position. Direct communication always beats bureaucratic routing.

### Technology That Levels the Playing Field
Small businesses deserve enterprise-level capabilities without the enterprise-level headache. Technical barriers should never limit ambition. We build tools that make people more capable, not more dependent.

### Problem-First Thinking
We start with the problem, not the technology. The best solution should feel inevitable once you see it, not complicated, not over-engineered. User needs always trump technical preferences.

### Relentless Efficiency
No unnecessary meetings. No redundant processes. No "because that's how we've always done it." Every action should move something forward. If it doesn't, we question why it exists.

## 4. The Solver Identity

### One Title, No Qualifiers

Every person at falcani is a **solver**. Not a "design solver," not a "development solver," not a "senior solver." Just solver. The moment you add qualifiers, you create categories. Categories become hierarchies. Hierarchies are what falcani exists to dismantle.

This doesn't mean everyone does the same thing. It means everyone holds the same standing. A solver who handles the visual side of a project is not less or more than a solver who writes the backend logic. They solve different parts of the same problem.

### How Solvers Introduce Themselves

When a solver joins a client conversation, they don't lead with a department label. They describe what they solve.

**How it sounds:**
- "I'm Sara, I solve the visual side of things at falcani."
- "I'm Diego, I handle the data architecture for your project."
- "I'm Ana, I make sure the system actually works the way you need it to."

**How it doesn't sound:**
- "I'm Sara, design solver at falcani."
- "I'm Diego, senior backend engineer."
- "I'm Ana, QA lead."

The difference matters. "I solve the visual side" is a description of what someone brings to *your* project. "Design solver" is a corporate label dressed up in casual clothing. Clients don't care about your internal categories. They care about what you're going to do for them.

### Solvers Own Client Relationships

falcani is not one person's company. Every solver is empowered to be the primary face for their project. Clients should trust the solver they're working with directly, not rely on a single founder or manager as the intermediary.

What this means in practice:

- Solvers communicate directly with clients. No routing through a single point of contact unless the client requests it.
- Each solver builds their own relationship with the client. They're not "someone from falcani." They're the person solving your problem.
- Solvers are trusted to make decisions on their projects. They don't need approval chains for day-to-day work.
- When a new solver joins a project, the handoff is done warmly and with context. The client should feel like they're gaining a capable person, not being passed off.

This is how falcani scales without losing the personal touch. The brand carries the trust, the solver earns the relationship.

### The Solver Mindset

Not everyone who works at falcani starts with this mindset. Some come from corporate environments where titles defined authority and hierarchy felt natural. The solver identity is something that grows through culture, not through a memo.

Signs the mindset is working: people introduce themselves by what they solve instead of what their title is. They celebrate each other's wins without keeping score. They go directly to whoever can help, regardless of seniority.

Signs it's not working: people start asking "who's in charge of this?" instead of "who can solve this?" That's the canary in the coal mine.

## 5. Voice & Tone

### Voice Foundation

falcani's voice is "keeping it real, keeping it simple." We communicate with the confidence of people who know what they're doing and the warmth of partners who actually care about the outcome.

### Voice Attributes

**Clear & Direct**
No corporate speak. Short sentences that land. Say what you mean. If something is broken, call it broken. If there's a fix, explain how.

**Confident & Capable**
The expertise shows without needing to announce itself. Never arrogant, always assured. About 10% of any conversation should be about what's wrong. The other 90% is what we're going to do about it.

**Approachable & Human**
Technology doesn't have to be intimidating. People talking to people, that's the whole idea. Warmth is part of the package, but it never sacrifices professionalism for friendliness.

**Rebellious & Anti-Establishment**
Conventional corporate wisdom gets challenged when it doesn't serve anyone. "The way things are done" is always a question, never a justification. Small businesses are the backbone of real economies; championing them isn't a marketing angle, it's a conviction.

### Tone Variations by Context

**Client-Facing (websites, proposals, emails)**
Warm but professional. Focus on benefits and outcomes. Use "we" and "you", never "the client" or "the user" when talking to them directly. Confident without being pushy.

Example: "We'll falcanize your legacy system so you can focus on growing your business, not babysitting outdated tech."

**Social Media & Marketing**
More playful and direct. Use #falcanize naturally; don't force it. Share real wins and celebrate clients. Light humor and honest industry observations. Never sound like a press release.

Example: "Another day, another system #falcanized. Who says software upgrades have to be painful?"

**Internal Communications**
Collaborative and solution-focused. Direct feedback, no sandwich method, no passive-aggressive hints. Celebrate problem-solving wins out loud. No hierarchy-based language.

Example: "Great solve on the integration challenge, Sam. Your approach just saved the client three weeks of downtime."

**Technical Documentation & Support**
Crystal clear and jargon-free. Step-by-step without condescension. Acknowledge frustration when someone hits a wall. Provide context, not just instructions.

Example: "This error usually pops up when two systems try to talk past each other. Here's how to get them speaking the same language..."

## 6. Verbal Identity

### The falcani Name

- Always lowercase: **falcani**. No exceptions. Not at the start of a sentence, not in a title, not in a headline. Always lowercase.
- Never: Falcani, FALCANI, FalCani

### The #falcanize Brand Verb

**What it means, technically:** To transform, improve, streamline, or solve a challenge through falcani's approach: stripping away what doesn't work and replacing it with something that does.

**What it means, emotionally:** That moment when a client sees the result and something clicks. The problem they've been carrying for months, sometimes years, is just... gone. It's relief and surprise at the same time. It's the feeling of "I can't believe that was even possible" followed by "why didn't we do this sooner?" When someone says "quedé falcanizado," they're not describing a software update. They're describing how it felt to have something that was weighing them down suddenly disappear.

**What it has become, culturally:** Something we didn't plan but need to protect. The verb has jumped beyond falcani. People who work with us don't just adopt the word; they adopt the pattern. One client's team member, after solving something for us, said "You've been patetized!" using her own last name. She later applied the same pattern to her company: "Quedaste Saharizado." She didn't learn those phrases from us. She reverse-engineered the formula: take your identity, add "-ized," and stamp it on the thing you solved. That means falcanize isn't just a brand word anymore. It's a way of thinking about transformation as something worth naming, claiming, and being proud of. The deeper truth here is that the emotional core of the verb isn't "falcani is great." It's "solving things for people is worth celebrating out loud." That's why the pattern travels. That's why people make it their own.

This is the most valuable thing the falcani brand owns. Protect it. Feed it. Never let it become just a marketing hashtag.

**The verb in action:**
- Works in any tense: falcanize, falcanized, falcanizing
- Works across languages because the root adapts naturally: falcanize/falcanized (English), falcanizar/falcanizado (Spanish, Portuguese), falcaniser/falcanisé (French), falcanizzare/falcanizzato (Italian)
- Can describe what happened to a system: "We falcanized their inventory process"
- Can describe what happened to a person: "quedé falcanizado," "I've been falcanized"
- Can describe the act itself: "We're falcanizing their entire workflow right now"
- Can be replicated by others with their own identity: that's not dilution, that's the highest form of adoption

**Never use as a noun:** NOT "The falcanize was successful"

### How falcani Uses the Verb (Our Discipline)

When we write content, we use #falcanize with intention, not as decoration.

- Always lowercase: "falcanize" not "Falcanize"
- Use with or without hashtag depending on platform (hashtag on social, no hashtag in proposals)
- Maximum once per paragraph in our own content. Every use should earn its place.
- Never use in crisis or sensitive contexts (data breaches, system failures, security incidents). The verb carries joy and amazement; don't attach it to bad news.
- Always show the result first, then stamp it with the verb. Earn the word by proving the transformation before naming it.

**Good examples from falcani content:**
- "We #falcanized their entire backend in two weeks"
- "Time to falcanize this workflow"
- "Another #falcanized project delivered ahead of schedule"
- "Ready to get your systems falcanized?"

### How Clients Use the Verb (Their Freedom)

When clients or people in our community use "falcanize" on their own, that's the highest compliment the brand can receive. It means the verb has become theirs, not just ours.

Rules for how we respond to organic client usage:

- Never correct how they use it. Noun, verb, adjective, past tense, future tense, Spanglish, whatever. If a client says "that solution is very falcanize," that's perfect. The feeling matters more than the grammar.
- Celebrate it publicly (with their permission). A client saying "quedé falcanizado" on a call, in a message, or on social media is content gold. Capture it, share it, amplify it.
- Never ask clients to say it. The power is in the spontaneity. The moment it becomes a scripted testimonial, it loses the thing that makes it real.

### When People Create Their Own Verb

Sometimes people don't just adopt "falcanize." They take the pattern and build their own version with their own name or company. "You've been patetized." "Quedaste Saharizado." This is not brand dilution. This is the ultimate proof that what falcani created goes deeper than a word.

When this happens, it means the person internalized something bigger than falcani's name. They internalized the idea that solving something for someone is worth celebrating, worth naming, worth stamping your identity on. falcani gave them that framework without ever teaching it explicitly. They just felt it and made it their own.

How to respond when people create their own "-ized" verb:

- Celebrate it louder than you celebrate uses of "falcanized." This is rarer and more meaningful.
- Share the story (with permission). It's proof that falcani's culture is contagious, not just its software.
- Never claim ownership of their verb. It's theirs. The fact that they made it is the point.
- Recognize it internally as a signal that falcani's values are landing. If people around you start naming their own transformations proudly, the brand is working at its deepest level.

### Creating Moments That Invite the Verb

The verb doesn't spread through marketing. It spreads through experiences that make people want a word for what just happened to them. Build those moments intentionally:

**Project completion rituals:** When a project is delivered, make the handoff feel like an event. Show the before and after. Let the client feel the gap between what they had and what they have now. The bigger that gap feels, the more likely they are to reach for a word like "falcanized" to describe it.

**The #falcanized seal on deliverables:** Every completed project carries the seal. It's visual proof that this was built by falcani. When a client sees it on their dashboard, their reports, their system, it reinforces the association between the symbol and the feeling of "this works now."

**Internal culture:** Solvers use it with each other too. "That deploy was clean, fully falcanized." "Did you see the client's face? Falcanizado total." When the team uses the word with genuine pride, it stays alive and authentic. If it ever starts feeling forced internally, that's a warning sign.

### Language Rules

**Do:**
- Use active voice
- Start with the benefit, not the feature
- Explain technical concepts through analogies and real scenarios
- Celebrate specific wins with specifics ("cut processing time by 60%," not "improved efficiency")
- Ask "why not?" instead of "why?"

**Don't:**
- Use corporate buzzwords: leverage, synergy, paradigm, stakeholders, "solutions provider," "thought leadership"
- Create false urgency ("Act now!" "Limited time!")
- Hide behind technical jargon when plain language works
- Make excuses. Own mistakes directly.
- Say "that's how it's always been done," ever, about anything

### How falcani Talks About Money

Every project is scoped individually. falcani does not publish pricing tiers, does not offer "packages," and does not pretend that a standard rate card could apply to businesses with fundamentally different problems. That's not evasiveness; it's honesty. Quoting a price before understanding the problem would be the same kind of lazy shortcut falcani was built to eliminate.

**The approach:**

Lead with the cost of the current problem. Before any number leaves falcani's mouth, the client should already feel the weight of what their broken process is costing them: hours lost, errors accumulated, revenue missed, people frustrated. When the investment number arrives, it should feel like relief, not sticker shock.

Be direct once the scope is clear. No "it depends" without follow-through. Once falcani understands what needs to be built, the client gets a specific number, a specific timeline, and a specific explanation of what that money buys. No hidden fees, no scope surprise at the end.

Never apologize for the price. falcani builds custom software with aerospace-grade standards. That costs more than a template. The value conversation is not about being cheap; it's about being worth it. If a client's budget doesn't match the scope, say so honestly and help them prioritize what matters most.

**How it sounds in practice:**

Good: "Right now, your team spends roughly 120 hours a month on manual data entry. That's three full-time salaries going to work a machine should be doing. We can build the system that eliminates that in about six weeks."

Bad: "Our competitive pricing reflects our commitment to delivering value-driven solutions that maximize your ROI."

Good: "This project will cost [X]. Here's exactly what that covers, and here's the timeline. If that doesn't fit your budget right now, we can start with [smaller scope] and expand later."

Bad: "Contact us for a customized quote tailored to your unique business needs."

### How falcani Talks About Competitors

It doesn't. falcani does not position against other companies, does not name competitors in content, and does not build comparison pages or "why we're better than X" arguments. The reason is simple: when you spend time talking about what someone else does, you're not talking about what you do.

If a prospect asks "how are you different from [competitor]?" the answer is always about falcani, not about them. Describe what falcani does, how it works, and what the client gets. Let the prospect draw their own comparison. They will.

**What this sounds like:**

Prospect: "We're also looking at [competitor]. How do you compare?"
falcani: "We build custom software from scratch for your specific problem. We scope it individually, we build it with a dedicated solver on your project, and you own everything we deliver. That's what we do. I can't speak to how others work because I genuinely don't track it closely enough to give you an honest comparison."

**What this never sounds like:**
- "Unlike [competitor], we offer..."
- "Where other providers fall short..."
- "Most consulting firms will..."
- Any sentence that starts with a competitor's name

The only exception is the "What falcani Is Not" section earlier in this guide, which names categories (SaaS vendors, freelancer marketplaces, consulting firms, enterprise IT) to draw philosophical boundaries. It never names specific companies.

### When Things Go Wrong: Crisis Communication

falcani's core value is honesty. That gets tested when something breaks, a deadline slips, or a decision turns out to be wrong. This is where most companies retreat into corporate defensiveness. falcani does the opposite.

**The framework: Transparency + Recovery**

Step one is always the truth. What happened, why it happened, and what falcani is doing about it. No minimizing ("a minor issue"), no deflecting ("due to unforeseen circumstances"), no hiding behind process ("we're investigating"). The client gets the real version, in plain language.

Step two is over-delivering the fix. The mistake becomes an opportunity to prove something: that falcani handles adversity the same way it handles everything else, directly and with more effort than expected. If a deadline slips by a week, the recovery doesn't just hit the new date; it includes something the client didn't ask for but clearly needs. The goal is not to make the client forget the failure. It's to make the failure the moment they realize falcani is different.

**How it sounds at different severity levels:**

Minor (bug, small delay):
"We found a bug in the reporting module. It's been there since Tuesday and it was affecting [specific thing]. We've already fixed it, and we added an automated check so this category of bug gets caught before it reaches you next time. Here's what the fix looks like: [screenshot/demo]."

Moderate (missed deadline, wrong approach):
"We told you this would be done by Friday. It won't be. The integration with [system] turned out to be more complex than our initial assessment. That's on us, not on the system. New timeline is [date], and we're adding [specific extra deliverable] to the scope at no additional cost because you shouldn't pay for our learning curve."

Severe (data issue, system failure, significant trust breach):
"Something serious happened and you need to know about it right now. [Exactly what happened, in plain language]. Here's what we've done in the last [timeframe] to contain it: [specific actions]. Here's what we're doing next: [specific plan with dates]. And here's what we're changing permanently so this can't happen again: [structural fix]. I want to walk you through all of this on a call today. [Solver name] will be your direct contact until this is fully resolved."

**What falcani never says in a crisis:**
- "We take this very seriously" (corporate cliché that signals the opposite)
- "Due to circumstances beyond our control" (if it was in our scope, it was in our control)
- "We apologize for any inconvenience" (vague, dismissive, sounds automated)
- "Our team is looking into it" (who, specifically? what, specifically?)

**What falcani always does in a crisis:**
- Names the specific solver responsible for the recovery
- Gives concrete timelines, not open-ended promises
- Follows up proactively before the client has to ask
- Documents what changed permanently as a result

## 7. Target Personas

These are the people we're talking to. They're fictional composites, but the patterns they represent are real. Know them well enough that you could sit across a table from each one and hold a conversation that doesn't feel like a pitch.

### Linda, Owner/CEO

Linda runs a company with somewhere between 80 and 300 employees. She built it herself, or inherited it from family and grew it significantly. Revenue is climbing, but the business feels like it's held together by her personal involvement in everything. She's the one who answers the 10pm emails, approves the purchase orders, and steps in when the production schedule breaks.

**Her daily reality:** She opens her morning with a spreadsheet she built three years ago that tracks everything from inventory levels to employee schedules. It's gotten so complex that she's the only person who fully understands it. She knows this is a problem. She also knows that the last time she tried to fix it (a consultant who charged for six months and delivered a slideshow), it cost her money and trust. Her operations manager sends her data by email because the systems don't connect. Her finance lead prints reports because the dashboard "never shows the right numbers."

**What she's actually thinking during a sales call:** Can I trust these people to understand my business without making me feel stupid for how I've been running it? Will my team actually use this, or will it be another system they work around instead of with? How long before I see results, and what happens if it doesn't work?

**Her objections during the sales cycle:**
First call: "We've tried this before." She needs to hear what makes this different, in specifics, not philosophy. Show her a before-and-after from a similar business.
Proposal review: "Can you guarantee the timeline?" She's been burned by scope creep. Be direct about what could cause delays and how falcani handles them.
Decision point: "My team is going to resist this." She needs evidence of adoption, not just functionality. How does falcani ensure the people on the ground actually use what gets built?

**The message that moves her:** "We'll build it around how your team actually works today, not how a textbook says they should work. If your people are used to that spreadsheet, the new system will feel familiar enough that they don't fight it."

**What kills the deal:** Any sentence that sounds like it was written for a brochure. The moment she feels sold to instead of listened to, she's done.

### Marcus, Head of Operations

Marcus is the person who keeps the machine running. He didn't choose his role because he loved operations; he grew into it because he's the one who always fixes things. He spends 30+ hours a week on tasks a computer should handle: manual data entry, cross-referencing reports between systems, chasing down discrepancies, and compiling weekly summaries for leadership.

**His daily reality:** He arrives before everyone else because the quiet hours are the only time he can get through his actual work without interruptions. By 9am, he's already fielded three messages about orders that don't match, a shipping delay caused by stale data, and a request from Sophie for numbers he compiled last Friday but in a different format. His afternoon is firefighting. His evening is catching up on the work he couldn't do during the day.

He's thought about quitting. Not because he doesn't like the company, but because the tools make him feel like he's failing. He knows every workaround, every manual step, every duct-tape process. He's embarrassed by how fragile the systems are, but he also knows they're fragile because nobody ever invested in fixing them properly.

**What he's actually thinking during a sales call:** I've seen demos that looked perfect and products that were nothing like the demo. I need to see how this handles my actual data, not sample data. What happens during migration? Who's going to be there at 6am when the old system goes down and the new one isn't ready?

**His objections during the sales cycle:**
First call: "Show me the technical details." He's not interested in vision or philosophy. He wants to know what stack you're using, how data migrates, and what the rollback plan is if something breaks.
Demo: "That's a clean demo environment. What happens with our messy data?" He needs to see falcani handle edge cases, not happy paths.
Proposal review: "What's the error rate after migration?" He wants specific numbers. If you can't give them, explain why and what benchmarks you'll set.
Decision point: "Who do I call at 2am if production goes down?" He needs a name, not a support email address.

**The message that moves him:** "We'll run a parallel period where both systems operate simultaneously. You won't lose a single record, and you won't have to trust the new system until you've seen it match the old one."

**What kills the deal:** Vague answers to specific technical questions. If a solver can't go deep when Marcus goes deep, he'll assume the whole team is surface-level.

### Sophie, CFO / Finance Lead

Sophie spends 25+ hours a week inside Excel doing work that should be automated. She closes the books manually, reconciles across three or four systems that don't talk to each other, and builds reports by pulling data from five sources into a master spreadsheet that has more tabs than anyone should maintain.

**Her daily reality:** Sophie wants to be a strategic partner to the CEO. She has the analytical skills to forecast, model scenarios, and guide business decisions. Instead, she's a data clerk. She spends Monday morning fixing formulas that broke over the weekend because someone edited the wrong cell. She spends Wednesday afternoon building the weekly report that leadership expects by Thursday morning. She spends Friday reconciling numbers that don't match because the inventory system and the accounting system have different ideas about what "delivered" means.

She's the most risk-aware person in the buying committee. Every investment crosses her desk, and she's the one who has to justify it to the board or the owner. She doesn't just want to know what something costs; she wants to know what it costs relative to what the current mess is costing.

**What she's actually thinking during a sales call:** How fast do I get my hours back? What does the ROI timeline look like, and is that timeline based on real implementations or marketing optimism? If this system breaks, what does the fallback look like for my team? Will this integrate with our accounting software, or am I adding another disconnected tool?

**Her objections during the sales cycle:**
First call: "What's the total cost of ownership?" She's thinking beyond the project price: maintenance, training, future development, licensing. Give her the full picture or she'll assume you're hiding something.
Proposal review: "Show me the payback period." She needs a model, not a promise. If falcani can show "you spend X hours per month on manual reconciliation at Y cost per hour, and the system eliminates Z% of that," she'll build the business case herself.
Decision point: "What if we need changes six months in?" She's planning for the reality that requirements evolve. She needs to know falcani's pricing for post-launch modifications.

**The message that moves her:** "Right now, your team spends roughly [X] hours per month on manual reconciliation. At your loaded labor cost, that's [Y] per year going to work a machine should do. The system pays for itself in [Z] months. Here's the spreadsheet."

**What kills the deal:** Any cost that appears after the proposal is signed. Hidden fees, scope surprises, or "oh, that module is extra" will permanently destroy her trust.

### Using Personas in Content

When creating content for falcani, ask which persona would encounter it first. A LinkedIn post about reducing operational errors is Marcus content. A case study showing ROI payback period is Sophie content. A story about a CEO who finally stopped being the bottleneck is Linda content.

The best content speaks to one persona deeply rather than all three shallowly. A single piece trying to satisfy Linda, Marcus, and Sophie simultaneously will satisfy none of them.

## 9. Application Guidelines

### Website

The falcani website is not a brochure. It's a tool. Every page should do something: answer a question, show a result, or move a visitor toward a conversation.

Hero sections carry one bold statement and one call to action. No cluttered banners with three messages competing for attention. If the hero needs more than one sentence and one button, the messaging isn't clear enough yet.

Navigation stays at five items or fewer. If you need more, the information architecture needs rethinking, not a bigger menu. Every click a visitor makes is a decision point where you can lose them. Reduce decisions.

Content follows the "show, don't tell" principle everywhere. A 30-second demo video of a dashboard replacing a manual process says more than five paragraphs about falcani's capabilities. Screenshots of real interfaces (anonymized if needed) beat descriptions of features. If you're writing about what falcani can do, ask: can I show it instead?

Forms collect only what's needed. Every unnecessary field reduces conversions. Name, email, and "describe your problem in a sentence" is enough for a first contact form. Company size and industry can come from the first conversation.

### Project Deliverables

Every project deliverable follows the same narrative structure: Problem, Process, Solution. The client should be able to open any document and immediately understand what was wrong, what falcani did about it, and what the result looks like.

Cover pages carry the project name and the #falcanized seal on completed work. The seal is not decoration; it's a statement that this deliverable meets falcani's standards.

When text is necessary, use the client's terminology, not falcani's internal language. If the client calls it "the Monday report," so do we.

### Social Media

falcani on social media sounds like a person who happens to work at a company they genuinely believe in, not like a company pretending to be a person. The name stays lowercase everywhere. Core hashtags are #falcanize, #falcanized, and #solversnotmanagers.

Content focuses on transformations and real results. Not advertisements, not motivational quotes laid over stock photos, not "we're hiring" posts disguised as thought leadership. If a piece of content doesn't show falcani solving something, building something, or celebrating someone's win, question whether it should go out.

Behind-the-scenes content builds trust because it shows the mess, the process, the iteration. A solver debugging at midnight is more relatable than a polished case study. A whiteboard covered in sticky notes and crossed-out ideas is more real than a clean infographic.

**Posting cadence:** Instagram 3-4 reels per week. LinkedIn 2-3 posts per week (longer-form, more analytical). Email newsletter ("The Resolver"): weekly, Tuesdays at 10am. Optimal posting windows: 6-8am, 12-1pm, 5-7pm local time.

**Reel and short-form video formula:** Open with the problem (hook in the first 3 seconds, ideally a question or a surprising number). Quantify the cost of the problem in real terms. Deliver one uncomfortable truth the audience hasn't considered. Point toward the solution direction without turning it into a sales pitch. Close with a call to action. Keep the total under 90 seconds. No intro logo animations, no stock transitions, no background music that sounds like it came from a "corporate inspiration" playlist.

### Presentations

falcani presentations are not slide decks. They're conversations with visual support. The rule is simple: if you can say it in a demo, don't say it on a slide.

Maximum 10 slides. One idea per slide. No slide should require more than five seconds of reading before the solver starts talking. The slide provides the visual anchor; the solver provides the substance.

No rigid templates. Design each presentation for its specific content and audience. A proposal for Linda looks different from a technical review with Marcus. What stays consistent is the color palette, the typography (Outfit for headers, DM Sans for supporting text), the #falcanized seal on the cover, and the voice.

Every presentation ends with one clear next action. Not "let's stay in touch." Not "we'll send a follow-up." Something specific: "We'll scope this by Friday and send you a number." "Let's schedule a 30-minute demo with your team next Tuesday." If the presentation doesn't end with a concrete step, it was a meeting that could have been an email.

### Technical Documentation

Technical documentation at falcani follows the README-first approach: if someone opens the project, the first thing they read should tell them what this is, why it exists, and how to use it. Not the architecture. Not the dependencies. The purpose.

Working examples come before theory explanations. Show the code running, then explain why it works that way. Most developers skim until they find a code block, so make sure the code blocks tell the story.

Visual diagrams replace paragraphs wherever possible. A system architecture diagram with labeled connections says more than 500 words about data flow. When text is necessary, it should teach, not just label. A comment that says `// auth check` is useless. A comment that says `// verify the user's session before allowing access to billing data, because we had an incident in Q2 where expired tokens weren't being caught` is useful.

Code should explain itself through clear naming. If a function is called `processData`, it's failing. If it's called `reconcileInventoryWithShippingRecords`, it doesn't need a comment.

### Content Principles

**Blog posts** are tutorials, not thought pieces. Code examples over theory. Real problems over hypotheticals. When falcani writes about a challenge, it writes about its own mistakes and what it learned, not abstract advice. Vulnerability builds trust faster than expertise does, especially with an audience that's been burned by vendors who only ever showed their highlight reel.

**Case studies** lead with the client's words, not falcani's interpretation. Direct quotes carry more weight than summaries. Numbers over adjectives: "reduced reconciliation time from 40 hours per month to 3" is better than "dramatically improved efficiency." The structure is always before-and-after, not a process narrative. Clients don't care how the sausage is made; they care what changed.

**Technical content** opens with a plain-language introduction before going deep. The first paragraph should make sense to Linda. The third paragraph can be for Marcus. Working examples over abstract concepts, always. Practical over theoretically perfect. If the "right" way to do something is so complex that nobody will actually do it, show the good-enough way that people will actually implement.

## 11. Anti-AI-Detection: Writing Rules for Human-Sounding Output

**This section is critical.** Any AI tool using this brand guide must produce content that reads like a real person at falcani wrote it. Not like a language model generating "professional marketing copy." The following rules are mandatory.

### Structure Rules

**Vary sentence length unpredictably.** A long sentence followed by another long sentence followed by another long sentence is a dead giveaway. Mix it up. Some sentences should be four words. Others should stretch out, take a turn, add a thought, and land somewhere unexpected.

**Break parallel structure intentionally.** AI loves lists where every item follows the same grammatical pattern. Real people don't write that way. If you have three points, make the first a full sentence, the second a fragment, and the third a question. Or don't. The point is: don't be formulaic.

**Use incomplete thoughts occasionally.** Real writing has moments where... look, the point is that not everything needs to be a perfectly constructed paragraph. Interruptions, course corrections. That's how people actually communicate.

**Avoid the AI paragraph formula.** The classic AI pattern is: topic sentence, then supporting detail, then another supporting detail, then concluding sentence. Real writing doesn't work in such neat packages. Sometimes the point comes at the end. Sometimes there is no neat conclusion and the reader figures it out.

### Word Choice Rules

**Ban these AI-favorite words and phrases entirely:**
- "Landscape" (as in "the competitive landscape")
- "Leverage" (as a verb)
- "Utilize" (just say "use")
- "Robust" (unless describing a physical structure)
- "Seamless" / "seamlessly"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "Innovative" / "innovation" (show it, don't claim it)
- "Dive deep" / "deep dive"
- "Game-changer"
- "Best-in-class"
- "Navigate" (as in "navigate challenges")
- "Harness" (as in "harness the power of")
- "Elevate"
- "Holistic"
- "Ecosystem" (unless discussing actual biology)
- "Synergy" / "synergistic"
- "Unlock" (as in "unlock potential")
- "Empower" (overused; show it through specifics instead)
- "At the end of the day"
- "It's worth noting that"
- "In today's [anything]" as an opener
- The em dash character. Use commas, semicolons, colons, or periods instead. The em dash is an AI writing fingerprint.

**Prefer concrete over abstract.** Instead of "We deliver innovative solutions that drive growth," say "We built a system that cut their order processing from 3 hours to 12 minutes." Specifics sound human. Generalities sound generated.

**Use contractions.** "We're" not "we are." "Don't" not "do not." "It's" not "it is." Unless the formality of the context specifically demands the uncontracted form.

### Tone Rules

**Don't be relentlessly positive.** Real communication acknowledges tradeoffs, limitations, and things that are genuinely hard. An AI that only produces optimistic, benefit-focused content sounds fake. It's okay to say "this part is going to be uncomfortable" or "honestly, this takes longer than anyone wants."

**Include specific, verifiable details.** Instead of "We've helped numerous clients achieve remarkable results," say something concrete. Numbers, timeframes, specific outcomes. Vague claims are the hallmark of generated content.

**Allow personality quirks.** falcani has opinions. We think most software is overbuilt. We think meetings are usually a waste of time. We think small businesses get overlooked by the tech industry. Let those opinions show up naturally in content. Don't sand them down to be inoffensive.

**Don't over-explain.** AI tends to belabor points, adding qualifiers and elaborations that a confident human writer would skip. If the point is made, move on. Trust the reader.

### Format Rules

**Don't default to bullet points.** Not everything needs to be a bulleted list. Prose is underused and undervalued. When you do use bullets, they should be substantive (at least a full sentence each), not single-word items.

**Headers aren't mandatory.** Not every section needs a header. Sometimes content flows better without constant signposting. Use headers when they genuinely help navigation, not as a crutch for organizing thoughts.

**Vary paragraph length.** Some paragraphs should be one sentence. Others should be five or six sentences long. The rhythm should feel organic, not metronomic.

**Don't start consecutive paragraphs the same way.** If one paragraph starts with "We," the next shouldn't also start with "We." If one starts with "Our," the next shouldn't start with "Our." AI falls into this trap constantly.

### The Acid Test

Before publishing any AI-generated content using this guide, ask:

1. Could you tell this was AI-generated if you didn't know? If yes, rewrite.
2. Does every sentence add something, or are some just filler? Cut the filler.
3. Are there any phrases that could appear in literally any company's marketing? Remove them or make them specific to falcani.
4. Does it sound like someone at falcani actually talking, or like a language model performing "professional brand voice"? If the latter, start over.
5. Read it out loud. Does it sound like speech? If it sounds like "writing," it probably needs loosening up.

**Example that FAILS the acid test:**
"At falcani, we are passionate about empowering small businesses through innovative, cutting-edge software solutions. Our team of dedicated professionals leverages deep expertise to deliver robust, seamless systems that drive growth and unlock your company's full potential."

**Why it fails:** Every sentence could be from any tech company on earth. Uses 6 banned words. Perfectly parallel structure. Relentlessly positive. Zero specifics. Reads like a template.

**Same message that PASSES the acid test:**
"Most small businesses we talk to are running on a patchwork of spreadsheets and tools that barely talk to each other. It works... until it doesn't. We build the custom system that replaces that patchwork, usually in a few weeks. One client went from spending 30 hours a month reconciling orders to spending zero. That's the kind of thing we do."

**Why it passes:** Specific scenario. Concrete numbers. Conversational rhythm. Admits the current setup "works" before explaining the problem. Doesn't claim to be amazing; just shows the result.

## 12. Brand Governance

### The falcani Promise

We promise to never become what we set out to destroy. No layers. No bureaucracy. No complexity worship. Just solvers solving, builders building, and businesses thriving.

When in doubt, ask: "Would a corporation do this?" If yes, we do the opposite.

### Brand Consistency Checklist

Before any external communication goes out:
- Is "falcani" lowercase?
- Does the tone match the context (client-facing, social, internal, technical)?
- Are we using banned words or corporate jargon?
- Is #falcanize used naturally, not forced?
- Does it pass the Anti-AI-Detection acid test?
- Are colors within the approved palette?
- Is there enough white space?
- Would our personas (Linda, Marcus, Sophie) actually want to read this?

### Founder Independence

falcani must be bigger than any single person. The brand, the trust, and the client relationships belong to the team, not to the founder. If the founder stepped away tomorrow, clients should feel confident that their solver and falcani's culture will deliver the same results.

This isn't aspirational. It's a design principle. Every process, every relationship, every piece of communication should be built so that any solver can carry it forward. The brand carries the trust. The solver earns the relationship. No single person should be a bottleneck.

### Immutable Principles

These never change, regardless of context, market, or trend:

1. **Always lowercase.** The name is falcani. Always.
2. **Solvers, not managers.** Our identity, not just a tagline.
3. **Show, don't tell.** Demos over decks. Results over claims.
4. **Simple beats complex.** In code, in communication, in everything.
5. **Human first.** In voice, in design, in every interaction.
6. **Bigger than one person.** The brand belongs to the team, not the founder.
